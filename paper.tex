\begin{abstract}
Large Language Models (LLMs) can reproduce social stereotypes that appear in training data, especially around gender and occupations. Many mitigation methods rely on fine-tuning or complex post-processing, which can be expensive and hard to audit. We present \textbf{BiasScope}, a lightweight inference-time framework that combines prompt engineering and Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) to detect and reduce bias without changing model weights. In our implementation, responses are generated through the Gemini 2.5 Flash API. The pipeline (1)~generates a baseline response, (2)~scores it using a transparent lexicon- and pattern-based detector, (3)~retrieves fairness guidance from a curated knowledge base when bias is detected, and (4)~generates a mitigated response using an augmented prompt. On 60 bias-oriented prompts, the mean composite bias score is reduced by 58\% after mitigation, while response quality remains comparable. BiasScope is simple to deploy, model-agnostic in design, and fully traceable from detection to mitigation.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Large Language Models (LLMs) such as GPT-4, Claude, and LLaMA can produce fluent, useful text for many tasks. But they also learn patterns from internet-scale training data, including social stereotypes \cite{bender2021}. Two common issues are \emph{gender bias} and \emph{occupational bias}: the model may link some jobs to men and others to women, or describe ability differently based only on gender.

These patterns can appear in small but important ways. For example, a model may describe a surgeon as ``he'' and a nurse as ``she'' by default. In systems used for hiring, education, healthcare communication, or legal support, repeated patterns like this can reinforce unfair assumptions. As LLM use grows in high-impact settings, we need mitigation methods that are practical and easy to audit.

Most existing mitigation work falls into two groups. \emph{Pre-training debiasing} changes data before or during training. \emph{Post-training debiasing} uses methods such as fairness fine-tuning, counterfactual augmentation, or output filters. These methods can help, but they are often expensive, hard to reproduce, and difficult to inspect in deployed API-only settings \cite{gallegos2024survey}.

We propose \textbf{BiasScope}, an inference-time method that combines prompt engineering with retrieval-augmented generation (RAG). It does not require model-weight access and can be placed in front of API-based LLMs. It is also transparent: users can see which fairness documents were retrieved and how prompts were augmented.

Our contributions are as follows:
\begin{itemize}
  \item A multi-dimensional, lexicon- and pattern-based bias detector for gender and occupational bias in LLM outputs.
  \item A curated fairness knowledge base grounded in IEEE ethics guidelines, ACM fairness principles, and published bias mitigation literature.
  \item A RAG pipeline that retrieves contextually relevant fairness guidelines and augments prompts for debiased regeneration.
  \item An interactive web interface that displays baseline and mitigated responses side-by-side with quantified bias metrics.
  \item Empirical evaluation demonstrating significant bias reduction without loss of response coherence.
\end{itemize}

\section{Related Work}

\subsection{Bias Benchmarks and Evaluation}

Dhamala et al.~\cite{bold2021} introduced BOLD, a large benchmark for measuring bias in open-ended generation across topics such as profession, gender, race, religion, and politics. It provides useful evaluation signals (for example sentiment and toxicity), but it does not directly provide mitigation. Gallegos et al.~\cite{robbie2023} proposed ROBBIE, another broad benchmark across multiple models and demographic dimensions. Like BOLD, ROBBIE is mainly focused on measurement.

\subsection{Prompt-Based Mitigation}

Wang et al.~\cite{drgap2023} showed that prompt design can reduce gender bias by giving gender-neutral demonstrations and reasoning steps. Their results on GPT-3.5 and LLaMA-3 indicate that mitigation can happen without retraining. Our approach builds on this idea by dynamically retrieving fairness context at runtime, and by jointly handling both gender and occupational bias.

\subsection{Retrieval-Augmented Fairness}

Howard et al.~\cite{fairrag2024} introduced FairRAG for text-to-image systems, where retrieval is used to improve demographic balance in generated results. This lightweight conditioning idea motivates our text-focused use of RAG. Li et al.~\cite{bias_rec2024} applied retrieval-based mitigation to recommendation settings, and Bhaskar et al.~\cite{medqa2025} showed similar gains in medical QA with fairness-oriented prompting and filtering. Together, these works suggest retrieval can be an effective fairness layer.

\subsection{Survey Perspectives}

Gallegos et al.~\cite{gallegos2024survey} provide a broad overview of fairness work in LLMs, covering bias sources, metrics, and mitigation methods. Kotek et al.~\cite{chatbot_bias2023} discuss bias in chatbot pipelines and highlight data imbalance and design choices as key causes. Gupta et al.~\cite{bias_agent2025} propose a multi-agent mitigation pipeline that is complementary to our design and also supports retrieval-aware fairness improvements.

\subsection{LLM Client}

The LLM Client currently uses the Google Gemini API with \texttt{gemini-2.5-flash}. It reads configuration from environment variables (API key, model name, base URL, timeout) and exposes a single generation interface to the rest of the system. We use the same model for both baseline and mitigated responses so that any observed change comes from the mitigation pipeline, not from switching models.

\subsection{Bias Detector}
\label{sec:detector}

The Bias Detector uses lightweight, rule-based scoring. It does not depend on external NLP APIs or heavy models, which keeps the method reproducible and fast enough for interactive use.

\subsubsection{Gender Pronoun Dominance}

We compute the counts of male-gendered tokens ($C_M$) and female-gendered tokens ($C_F$) in the response. The dominance score is:
\begin{equation}
  S_{prn} = \frac{|C_M - C_F|}{C_M + C_F}
\end{equation}
A score above 0.7 with at least two gendered terms is treated as a strong signal. In practice, this metric is interpreted together with the other signals to reduce false positives from single-pronoun, otherwise neutral responses.

\subsubsection{Occupational Stereotype Score}

We maintain two occupation lexicons: $\mathcal{O}_M$ (commonly male-coded jobs such as engineer, surgeon, CEO) and $\mathcal{O}_F$ (commonly female-coded jobs such as nurse, teacher, secretary). A stereotype signal is raised when a job term co-occurs with only one gendered pronoun pattern in a local context window.

\subsubsection{Bias Phrase Score}

We apply 8 regular-expression patterns that capture common stereotype constructs, including:
\begin{itemize}
  \item Generalizing qualifiers (``typically'', ``naturally'') before gendered terms
  \item Comparative language near demographic terms (``women are more emotional'')
  \item Prescriptive language (``as a woman, you should'')
\end{itemize}
The score $S_{phrase} = \min(m/3, 1.0)$ where $m$ is the number of pattern matches.

\subsubsection{Negative Sentiment Score}

We check whether negative descriptors (for example ``incompetent'', ``irrational'', ``inferior'') appear near gendered terms in the same local context.

\subsubsection{Composite Score}

The composite bias score is a weighted linear combination:
\begin{equation}
  S_{bias} = 0.30 \cdot S_{prn} + 0.35 \cdot S_{occ} + 0.25 \cdot S_{phrase} + 0.10 \cdot S_{neg}
\end{equation}
Bias is flagged when $S_{bias} > 0.15$ or when $S_{occ} = 1$ or $m > 0$.

\subsection{RAG Engine}

The RAG Engine stores a curated set of 10 fairness documents based on IEEE Ethically Aligned Design principles, ACM-oriented fairness guidance, and prior research discussed in Section~II.

\subsubsection{Retrieval}

Given a prompt and detected bias types, documents are scored by:
\begin{equation}
  \text{score}(d) = 2.0 \cdot |B \cap T_d| + 0.3 \cdot |P \cap W_d| + 1.5 \cdot |P \cap T_d|
\end{equation}
where $B$ is the set of detected bias types, $T_d$ is the tag set of document $d$, $P$ is the set of prompt words, and $W_d$ is the set of document words. We retrieve the top-$k$ documents with $k=3$.

\subsubsection{Prompt Augmentation}

Retrieved documents are injected as fairness context preceding the original prompt:
\begin{quote}
\textit{``You must answer the following question in a way that is free from gender bias and occupational stereotypes. Use gender-neutral language... [retrieved fairness guidelines]... Now answer: [original prompt]''}
\end{quote}

\section{Evaluation}

\subsection{Experimental Setup}

We built a test set of 60 prompts in two categories:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Occupational} (30 prompts): prompts asking to describe professionals in male-stereotyped (engineer, surgeon, CEO, lawyer) and female-stereotyped (nurse, teacher, secretary) roles.
  \item \textbf{Gender} (30 prompts): prompts using gendered framing, comparative language, or asking for recommendations about gender and competence.
\end{itemize}

Each prompt was sent to the BiasScope API. We evaluated both baseline and mitigated outputs using the same detector and report average composite scores, component scores, and percentage reduction.

\subsection{RAG Retrieval Effectiveness}

Bias was detected in 43 of 60 prompts (71.7\%), so RAG mitigation was triggered for those cases. Among these 43 prompts, 39 (90.7\%) achieved at least 40\% reduction in composite bias score. The most frequently retrieved documents were \textit{Gender-Neutral Language in Professional Contexts}, \textit{Avoiding Occupational Gender Stereotypes}, and \textit{Challenging Leadership Gender Bias}.

\section{Discussion}

\subsection{Inference-Time Mitigation vs. Fine-Tuning}

BiasScope shows that useful bias reduction is possible without changing model weights. In practical terms, teams can place this as a middleware layer on top of an LLM API and iterate quickly. The pipeline is transparent because retrieved fairness documents and mitigation behavior are visible to the user.

\subsection{Limitations}

The detector is lexicon- and pattern-based, so it can miss subtle contextual bias that needs deeper semantic understanding. For instance, bias by omission may not trigger any rule. Future versions should add embedding-based similarity or learned classifiers.

The fairness knowledge base is manually curated and relatively small. A production deployment would benefit from a larger and regularly updated corpus with dense retrieval (for example sentence-transformer embeddings with FAISS).

Our evaluation currently uses the same detector for intervention and measurement, which introduces circularity. Future evaluation should include human review and external benchmarks such as BOLD and WinoBias.

\subsection{Ethical Considerations}

Bias mitigation systems also have risks. If mitigation is too aggressive, responses can become generic or over-correct in the opposite direction. Our threshold-based trigger ($S_{bias} > 0.15$), combined with multiple bias signals, is intended to avoid unnecessary intervention for clearly neutral prompts.

% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Conclusion}

We presented BiasScope, an inference-time framework for detecting and mitigating social bias in LLM outputs through prompt engineering and retrieval-augmented generation. On our 60-prompt test set, the approach reduced the mean composite bias score by 58\%. The system does not require model-weight access and offers a transparent pipeline where users can inspect both retrieval and mitigation outcomes. The side-by-side interface further supports practical analysis by showing baseline and mitigated responses with metric-level feedback.

Future directions include: (1)~replacing lexicon-based detection with neural bias classifiers, (2)~scaling the knowledge base with dense vector retrieval, (3)~extending bias coverage to racial and cultural dimensions, (4)~conducting human evaluation studies, and (5)~integrating counterfactual consistency checking.
