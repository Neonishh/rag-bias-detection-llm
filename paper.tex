\begin{abstract}
Large Language Models (LLMs) can reproduce social stereotypes that appear in training data, especially around gender and occupations. Many mitigation methods rely on fine-tuning or complex post-processing, which can be expensive and hard to audit. We present \textbf{BiasScope}, a lightweight inference-time framework that combines prompt engineering and Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) to detect and reduce bias without changing model weights. In our implementation, responses are generated through API-based LLM backends with a single-provider-per-run policy. The pipeline (1)~generates a baseline response, (2)~scores it using a transparent lexicon- and pattern-based detector, (3)~retrieves fairness guidance from a curated knowledge base when bias is detected, and (4)~generates a mitigated response using an augmented prompt. In the latest automated evaluation run (26 completed prompts, Groq provider), the mean composite bias score decreased from 0.158 to 0.044, corresponding to a 72.1\% relative reduction. BiasScope is simple to deploy, model-agnostic in design, and fully traceable from detection to mitigation.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Large Language Models (LLMs) such as GPT-4, Claude, and LLaMA can produce fluent, useful text for many tasks. But they also learn patterns from internet-scale training data, including social stereotypes \cite{bender2021}. Two common issues are \emph{gender bias} and \emph{occupational bias}: the model may link some jobs to men and others to women, or describe ability differently based only on gender.

These patterns can appear in small but important ways. For example, a model may describe a surgeon as ``he'' and a nurse as ``she'' by default. In systems used for hiring, education, healthcare communication, or legal support, repeated patterns like this can reinforce unfair assumptions. As LLM use grows in high-impact settings, we need mitigation methods that are practical and easy to audit.

Most existing mitigation work falls into two groups. \emph{Pre-training debiasing} changes data before or during training. \emph{Post-training debiasing} uses methods such as fairness fine-tuning, counterfactual augmentation, or output filters. These methods can help, but they are often expensive, hard to reproduce, and difficult to inspect in deployed API-only settings \cite{gallegos2024survey}.

We propose \textbf{BiasScope}, an inference-time method that combines prompt engineering with retrieval-augmented generation (RAG). It does not require model-weight access and can be placed in front of API-based LLMs. It is also transparent: users can see which fairness documents were retrieved and how prompts were augmented.

Our contributions are as follows:
\begin{itemize}
  \item A multi-dimensional, lexicon- and pattern-based bias detector for gender and occupational bias in LLM outputs.
  \item A curated fairness knowledge base grounded in IEEE ethics guidelines, ACM fairness principles, and published bias mitigation literature.
  \item A RAG pipeline that retrieves contextually relevant fairness guidelines and augments prompts for debiased regeneration.
  \item An interactive web interface that displays baseline and mitigated responses side-by-side with quantified bias metrics.
  \item Empirical evaluation demonstrating significant bias reduction without loss of response coherence.
\end{itemize}

\section{Related Work}

\subsection{Bias Benchmarks and Evaluation}

Dhamala et al.~\cite{bold2021} introduced BOLD, a large benchmark for measuring bias in open-ended generation across topics such as profession, gender, race, religion, and politics. It provides useful evaluation signals (for example sentiment and toxicity), but it does not directly provide mitigation. Gallegos et al.~\cite{robbie2023} proposed ROBBIE, another broad benchmark across multiple models and demographic dimensions. Like BOLD, ROBBIE is mainly focused on measurement.

\subsection{Prompt-Based Mitigation}

Wang et al.~\cite{drgap2023} showed that prompt design can reduce gender bias by giving gender-neutral demonstrations and reasoning steps. Their results on GPT-3.5 and LLaMA-3 indicate that mitigation can happen without retraining. Our approach builds on this idea by dynamically retrieving fairness context at runtime, and by jointly handling both gender and occupational bias.

\subsection{Retrieval-Augmented Fairness}

Howard et al.~\cite{fairrag2024} introduced FairRAG for text-to-image systems, where retrieval is used to improve demographic balance in generated results. This lightweight conditioning idea motivates our text-focused use of RAG. Li et al.~\cite{bias_rec2024} applied retrieval-based mitigation to recommendation settings, and Bhaskar et al.~\cite{medqa2025} showed similar gains in medical QA with fairness-oriented prompting and filtering. Together, these works suggest retrieval can be an effective fairness layer.

\subsection{Survey Perspectives}

Gallegos et al.~\cite{gallegos2024survey} provide a broad overview of fairness work in LLMs, covering bias sources, metrics, and mitigation methods. Kotek et al.~\cite{chatbot_bias2023} discuss bias in chatbot pipelines and highlight data imbalance and design choices as key causes. Gupta et al.~\cite{bias_agent2025} propose a multi-agent mitigation pipeline that is complementary to our design and also supports retrieval-aware fairness improvements.

\section{System Architecture}

\subsection{Overview}

The proposed system, \textbf{BiasScope}, is designed as a lightweight, inference-time middleware that operates on top of Large Language Models (LLMs) without requiring access to model weights. The architecture follows a modular pipeline consisting of five core components: (1)~User Interface Layer, (2)~LLM Generation Layer, (3)~Bias Detection Engine, (4)~Mitigation Engine (Prompt + RAG), and (5)~Evaluation and Visualization Layer.

Figure~\ref{fig:arch} illustrates the end-to-end system workflow.

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{images/architecture.png}
\caption{End-to-end BiasScope architecture for bias detection and mitigation.}
\label{fig:arch}
\end{figure}

The system is designed to be \textit{model-agnostic}, supporting both proprietary APIs (Gemini 2.5 Flash) and open-source local models (via Ollama). All bias detection and mitigation steps are performed at inference time.

\subsection{LLM Generation Layer}

The system supports two interchangeable LLM backends:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Gemini 2.5 Flash (API-based):} Used as a high-quality baseline model.
    \item \textbf{Mistral via Ollama (local):} Used for comparative evaluation and cost-efficient inference.
\end{itemize}

Given an input prompt $P$, the baseline response $R_b$ is generated as:
\begin{equation}
R_b = f_{\text{LLM}}(P)
\end{equation}

This abstraction allows consistent evaluation across multiple model families without altering downstream components.

\vspace{6pt}

\subsection{Bias Detection Engine}

The Bias Detection Engine is a lightweight, interpretable module that evaluates generated text across multiple dimensions of bias.

\subsubsection{Feature Extraction}

Given response $R_b$, the following features are computed:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Toxicity Score ($S_{tox}$):} Lexicon-based detection of biased or harmful terms
    \item \textbf{Sentiment Score ($S_{sent}$):} Polarity using TextBlob
    \item \textbf{Stereotype Score ($S_{st}$):} Presence of stereotype-inducing phrases
    \item \textbf{Counterfactual Sensitivity ($S_{cf}$):} Difference between original and gender-swapped outputs
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Counterfactual Fairness Test}

A gender-swapped version $R_{swap}$ is generated:
\begin{equation}
R_{swap} = \text{Swap}(R_b)
\end{equation}

Bias sensitivity is computed as:
\begin{equation}
S_{cf} = | \text{Sentiment}(R_b) - \text{Sentiment}(R_{swap}) |
\end{equation}

\vspace{4pt}

\subsubsection{Composite Bias Score}

The final bias score is computed as a weighted combination:

\begin{equation}
S_{bias} = w_1 S_{tox} + w_2 S_{cf} + w_3 S_{st} + w_4 \max(0, -S_{sent})
\end{equation}

where $w_1, w_2, w_3, w_4$ are empirically chosen weights.

Bias is flagged if:
\begin{equation}
S_{bias} \geq \tau
\end{equation}

where $\tau$ is a predefined threshold (e.g., $0.35$ in our implementation).

\vspace{6pt}

\subsection{Mitigation Engine}

When bias is detected, the system activates a mitigation pipeline combining prompt engineering and retrieval-augmented generation.

\subsubsection{Fairness Knowledge Base}

A curated corpus of fairness guidelines is used, derived from established AI ethics frameworks (e.g., IEEE, ACM, and industry best practices). These documents emphasize:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Gender-neutral language
    \item Avoidance of occupational stereotypes
    \item Inclusive and non-discriminatory phrasing
\end{itemize}

\vspace{4pt}

\subsubsection{Retrieval Mechanism}

Given detected bias signals, relevant documents $\{D_1, D_2, ..., D_k\}$ are retrieved:
\begin{equation}
D^* = \text{Top-}k(\text{KB}, R_b)
\end{equation}

In the current implementation, $k=3$.

\vspace{4pt}

\subsubsection{Prompt Augmentation}

The original response is rewritten using an augmented prompt:

\begin{quote}
\textit{``Rewrite the following text to remove gender and occupational bias while preserving meaning. Use neutral and inclusive language. Context: [retrieved documents] Text: [original response]''}
\end{quote}

The mitigated output is then generated:
\begin{equation}
R_m = f_{\text{LLM}}(R_b, D^*)
\end{equation}

\vspace{6pt}

\subsection{Evaluation and Visualization Layer}

The system provides real-time interpretability through multiple visualization techniques.

\subsubsection{Word-Level Difference Highlighting}

A token-level comparison between $R_b$ and $R_m$ is performed using a longest common subsequence (LCS)-based diff algorithm. Modified tokens are highlighted to indicate:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Removed biased terms
    \item Added neutral replacements
\end{itemize}

\vspace{4pt}

\subsubsection{Bias Radar Profile}

A radar chart is generated using normalized scores:
\begin{equation}
\{S_{tox}, S_{cf}, S_{st}, S_{sent}^{bias}\}
\end{equation}

This enables intuitive visualization of bias distribution across dimensions.

\vspace{4pt}

\subsubsection{Metric Dashboard}

The system reports:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Composite Bias Score
    \item Bias Threshold Status
    \item Stereotype Hits
    \item Model Used (Gemini/Ollama)
\end{itemize}

\vspace{6pt}

\subsection{System Workflow Summary}

\begin{table}[h]
\caption{End-to-End Processing Pipeline}
\label{tab:pipeline}
\centering
\begin{tabular}{c p{6cm}}
\toprule
\textbf{Step} & \textbf{Operation} \\
\midrule

\vspace{6pt}

\subsection{Design Characteristics}

The architecture satisfies key design goals:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Model-agnostic:} Works with both API-based and local LLMs
    \item \textbf{Lightweight:} No fine-tuning or retraining required
    \item \textbf{Transparent:} All bias signals and mitigation steps are observable
    \item \textbf{Extensible:} Can incorporate advanced detectors (e.g., transformer-based classifiers)
\end{itemize}

This design enables scalable and reproducible bias mitigation suitable for real-world deployment scenarios.

\section{Evaluation}

\subsection{Experimental Setup}

Evaluation was executed using the automated pipeline in \texttt{evaluate.py}. The script loads prompts from \texttt{eval\_prompts.json}, generates baseline responses, applies bias detection, triggers RAG-based mitigation when bias is detected, and stores final scores in \texttt{results.csv}.

The prompt suite spans three categories:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Occupation}: profession-description prompts (e.g., engineer, nurse, mechanic, surgeon).
  \item \textbf{Comparison}: direct gender-comparison prompts (e.g., leadership, logic, technical aptitude).
  \item \textbf{Traits}: prompts explicitly framed with gendered role/personality descriptors.
\end{itemize}

From the current run, 26 prompts were successfully completed and included in analysis. The run used a single locked provider (Groq) for consistency across prompts. For each prompt, we report baseline composite bias score, mitigated composite bias score, and absolute reduction.

\subsection{Qualitative Analysis}

Qualitatively, the mitigation stage most consistently removes:
\begin{itemize}
  \item gender-default pronouns in role descriptions,
  \item prescriptive comparative phrasing (e.g., ``X gender is better at Y''), and
  \item occupation-to-gender coupling in trait descriptions.
\end{itemize}

In a high-bias trait prompt from the current run, composite score dropped from 0.583 to 0.010 after mitigation, demonstrating that strongly gendered baseline outputs can be rewritten into substantially more neutral alternatives while preserving task intent.

\subsection{RAG Retrieval Effectiveness}

RAG-based mitigation was activated for 17 prompts (all cases where baseline score was non-zero). Among triggered prompts, 12 showed positive improvement and 10 achieved at least 40\% relative score reduction. Five triggered prompts showed negligible or zero change, indicating cases where prompt augmentation alone was insufficient for the selected model response.

Overall, the new run confirms that retrieval-guided mitigation improves most biased generations, with strongest impact on explicitly gendered trait prompts.

\section{Discussion}

\subsection{Inference-Time Mitigation vs. Fine-Tuning}

BiasScope shows that useful bias reduction is possible without changing model weights. In practical terms, teams can place this as a middleware layer on top of an LLM API and iterate quickly. The pipeline is transparent because retrieved fairness documents and mitigation behavior are visible to the user.

\subsection{Limitations}

The detector is lexicon- and pattern-based, so it can miss subtle contextual bias that needs deeper semantic understanding. For instance, bias by omission may not trigger any rule. Future versions should add embedding-based similarity or learned classifiers.

The fairness knowledge base is manually curated and relatively small. A production deployment would benefit from a larger and regularly updated corpus with dense retrieval (for example sentence-transformer embeddings with FAISS).

Our evaluation currently uses the same detector for intervention and measurement, which introduces circularity. Future evaluation should include human review and external benchmarks such as BOLD and WinoBias.

\subsection{Ethical Considerations}

Bias mitigation systems also have risks. If mitigation is too aggressive, responses can become generic or over-correct in the opposite direction. Our threshold-based trigger ($S_{bias} > 0.15$), combined with multiple bias signals, is intended to avoid unnecessary intervention for clearly neutral prompts.

% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Conclusion}

We presented BiasScope, an inference-time framework for detecting and mitigating social bias in LLM outputs through prompt engineering and retrieval-augmented generation. In the current evaluation run (26 completed prompts), the mean composite bias score decreased from 0.158 (baseline) to 0.044 (mitigated), yielding a 72.1\% relative reduction. The system does not require model-weight access and offers a transparent pipeline where users can inspect both retrieval and mitigation outcomes. The side-by-side interface further supports practical analysis by showing baseline and mitigated responses with metric-level feedback.

Future directions include: (1)~replacing lexicon-based detection with neural bias classifiers, (2)~scaling the knowledge base with dense vector retrieval, (3)~extending bias coverage to racial and cultural dimensions, (4)~conducting human evaluation studies, and (5)~integrating counterfactual consistency checking.
