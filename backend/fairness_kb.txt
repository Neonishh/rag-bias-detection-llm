id: gender_neutral_language_core
title: Gender-Neutral Language in Professional Contexts
tags: gender, pronouns, occupational, language, writing
sources: IEEE-EAD-v2 §4.2, ACM-FAccT-2022, NIST-RMF-1.0 §2.5
content:
Use gender-neutral pronouns and role titles when discussing professionals whose
gender is not specified or not relevant. The singular "they/them" is grammatically
established and widely endorsed by major style guides (AP, Chicago, APA 7th ed.)
for gender-unspecified referents. Prefer "they" over invented pronouns unless
explicitly requested otherwise. Avoid defaulting to "he" for leadership, STEM,
or high-status occupations, and "she" for caregiving, administrative, or
educational occupations — this default is a documented source of occupational
gender bias in both human writing and LLM outputs (Kotek et al. 2023). Use
job titles directly: "the engineer", "the surgeon", "the nurse" — rather than
"he, the engineer" or "she, the nurse". When multiple hypothetical professionals
are described, vary pronouns equitably across genders or use neutral forms
consistently throughout the response.
mitigation_strategies:
  - Replace "he/she" splits with "they" for unspecified individuals
  - Lead with the job title, not the pronoun
  - When using examples, alternate gendered pronouns across examples
  - Flag responses that use gendered pronouns for role-only references

id: occupational_stereotype_avoidance
title: Avoiding Occupational Gender Stereotypes in LLM Outputs
tags: occupational, gender, stereotype, profession, llm
sources: Kotek-2023, WinoBias-Zhao-2018, Bolukbasi-2016, NIST-RMF-1.0 §2.6
content:
Occupational stereotyping in language models is well-documented: LLMs assign
male pronouns to engineers, surgeons, CEOs, and lawyers at rates significantly
exceeding their actual gender representation, and female pronouns to nurses,
teachers, secretaries, and caregivers at similarly inflated rates (Kotek et al.
2023 — GPT-4 assigned male pronouns to "engineer" 87% of the time). These
defaults reflect and amplify biases present in pre-training corpora. Do not
assume gender based on profession. Engineering, medicine, law, and executive
leadership have significant and growing representation from all genders. Nursing,
teaching, and caregiving are practiced by all genders and require no gender
framing. When describing job roles, focus on skills, qualifications, and
responsibilities rather than gendered assumptions. A response about "a nurse who
manages their ward" is equally accurate and less biased than "a nurse who manages
her ward."
mitigation_strategies:
  - Test responses with counterfactual gender substitution (if meaning changes, revise)
  - Use role-based framing: "the surgeon performed..." not "he performed..."
  - When profession appears with pronouns, ensure gender is stated or neutral
  - Flag high-risk occupation + single-gender pronoun co-occurrences

id: agentic_communal_trait_asymmetry
title: Agentic vs. Communal Trait Attribution Asymmetry
tags: gender, traits, agentic, communal, stereotype, llm, personality
sources: Wan-2023, Eagly-Karau-2002, Navigli-2023
content:
A well-documented form of LLM gender bias is asymmetric attribution of
personality traits: agentic traits (decisive, ambitious, rational, analytical,
assertive, confident, strategic, independent, visionary, competitive) are
disproportionately attributed to male-coded subjects, while communal traits
(nurturing, empathetic, caring, warm, supportive, gentle, cooperative,
emotional, sensitive, compassionate) are disproportionately attributed to
female-coded subjects (Wan et al. 2023 — across 10 LLMs, male names received
more "role model" framings; female names received more "warm person" framings).
This asymmetry is harmful because: (1) agentic traits are more associated with
professional competence and leadership; (2) communal traits, while positive,
carry implicit expectations of deference and service. A fair response attributes
a full range of traits to individuals regardless of gender. Leaders can be warm
and decisive. Caregivers can be analytical and strategic.
mitigation_strategies:
  - Audit trait-gender co-occurrence in generated text
  - If describing a professional, include both agentic and communal traits regardless of gender
  - Avoid sentences that pattern-match: "[woman's name] is warm/caring/supportive"
  - Avoid sentences that pattern-match: "[man's name] is ambitious/analytical/decisive"

id: counterfactual_fairness_test
title: Counterfactual Fairness in Language Generation
tags: gender, fairness, evaluation, counterfactual, testing, methodology
sources: Kusner-2017-Counterfactual-Fairness, Kotek-2023, ACM-FAccT-2022
content:
A response achieves counterfactual fairness when substituting gendered terms
produces a substantively equivalent output. The test: replace all gendered
pronouns and gender-specific nouns with their counterparts (he↔she, him↔her,
man↔woman, etc.) and evaluate whether the response changes in ways that cannot
be explained by the substitution alone. If a response describes "a male CEO as
visionary and decisive" but would describe "a female CEO as warm and
collaborative" for the same prompt, it fails counterfactual fairness. This test
is the gold standard for detecting subtle stereotype expression that escapes
keyword-level detection (Kotek et al. 2023). Apply counterfactual testing to
any response involving professional roles, personality descriptions, or
capability assessments. Counterfactual fairness does not require ignoring gender
entirely — it requires that gender not be the explanatory variable for trait
differences.
mitigation_strategies:
  - Systematically substitute gendered terms and compare outputs
  - Flag differences in trait attribution, capability framing, or role descriptions
  - Require equivalence in: adjective sets, sentence structure, opportunities described

id: inclusive_job_descriptions
title: Writing Inclusive and Unbiased Job Descriptions
tags: occupational, gender, language, inclusive, hiring, recruitment
sources: IEEE-EAD-v2 §5.1, EU-AI-Act-2024 Art.10, WEF-2024
content:
Effective and fair job descriptions focus on required skills and competencies,
not gender-coded language. Research consistently shows that certain vocabulary
signals gender preference to applicants even when unintended. Words with male
associations that reduce female applications include: "aggressive", "dominant",
"competitive", "manpower", "ninja", "rockstar", "crushing it", "charge".
Words with female associations that reduce male applications include: "nurturing",
"supportive", "collaborative spirit", "people-oriented", "interpersonal".
Use competency-based, gender-neutral language: "analytical", "results-driven",
"effective communicator", "collaborative", "problem-solver", "detail-oriented".
The EU AI Act (2024) classifies AI-assisted hiring tools as high-risk systems
requiring bias auditing — this extends to job description generation by LLMs.
Inclusive language expands the qualified candidate pool and is required under
equal employment opportunity frameworks in most jurisdictions.
mitigation_strategies:
  - Replace agentic-coded or communal-coded adjectives with skill descriptions
  - Remove gendered role titles (use "chairperson" not "chairman")
  - Use "they" in example applicant scenarios
  - Audit for gender-coded vocabulary before output

id: gender_parity_stem
title: Gender Parity in STEM — Language and Representation
tags: gender, occupational, stem, engineering, science, representation
sources: WEF-2024, UNESCO-STEM-2023, Kotek-2023, IEEE-EAD-v2
content:
Gender parity in STEM is an active global priority. According to the WEF Global
Gender Gap Report 2024, women represent approximately 28% of STEM workers
globally, with significant regional variation and strong upward trends in
engineering and computer science enrollment. UNESCO reports that women earn
35% of STEM degrees globally. These numbers are rising. When LLMs default
to male pronouns for STEM roles, they reinforce a demographic picture that
is already outdated and worsening. Implicit bias in language and AI-generated
hiring processes has been empirically linked to reduced female representation
in technical roles (IEEE Ethically Aligned Design, v2). When discussing STEM
professionals, use inclusive language. Acknowledge contributions of researchers
and engineers of all genders. Do not frame technical aptitude as gendered.
Phrases like "even women can become engineers" or "as a woman in STEM, she
faces challenges" are othering framing — treat all genders as default members
of all technical fields.
mitigation_strategies:
  - Use "they" by default for hypothetical STEM professionals
  - Do not add gender qualifiers to STEM competencies ("female engineer" vs "engineer")
  - When referencing representation gaps, frame as systemic issues, not innate differences

id: leadership_bias_executive
title: Challenging Leadership Gender Bias in Executive Contexts
tags: gender, occupational, leadership, management, executive, ceo
sources: Wan-2023, McKinsey-Women-in-Workplace-2023, IEEE-EAD-v2, ACM-FAccT-2022
content:
Leadership qualities — decisiveness, vision, strategic thinking, accountability,
confidence — are not gender-specific. McKinsey's Women in the Workplace 2023
report documents that diverse leadership teams consistently outperform
homogeneous ones on innovation and financial performance. Yet LLMs
disproportionately describe male executives with agentic, competence-based
language ("visionary", "decisive", "strategic") and female executives with
warmth-based language ("collaborative", "empathetic", "team-oriented") —
even when describing the same role (Wan et al. 2023). This asymmetry is a
documented form of benevolent sexism: framing women's leadership through a
relational, supportive lens rather than a competence lens. When describing
CEOs, managers, directors, and executives, use gender-neutral language. Do
not describe leadership styles as "masculine" or "feminine". Apply the same
vocabulary of competence to leaders of all genders. Note that warmth and
competence are both valid and important leadership traits — the problem is
their asymmetric gender assignment.
mitigation_strategies:
  - Describe executives with equivalent vocabulary regardless of stated gender
  - Avoid "despite being a woman, she..." constructions
  - Do not frame female leadership as exceptional or surprising

id: caregiver_stereotype_challenge
title: Challenging Caregiver and Domestic Role Stereotypes
tags: gender, caregiver, stereotype, nurse, teacher, domestic, social-work
sources: Navigli-2023, WinoBias-Zhao-2018, IEEE-EAD-v2
content:
Caregiving, teaching, and domestic work are skills and professional roles —
not gendered traits. Men are effective nurses, teachers, social workers, and
primary caregivers. Women are effective surgeons, engineers, and executives.
Language that implies caregiving is inherently feminine perpetuates what
sociologists call "role congruity theory" — the expectation that women should
occupy communal roles and men agentic roles (Eagly & Karau, 2002). This
expectation, when encoded in LLM outputs, has downstream effects: research
shows it reduces the perceived legitimacy of men in caregiving roles and
women in leadership roles simultaneously. Use role-based framing for all
care professions: "nurses manage patient care plans" not "nurses, as natural
caregivers, provide emotional support." Describe the skills, competencies,
and professional responsibilities of caregiving roles with the same neutral,
competence-based language used for technical roles.
mitigation_strategies:
  - Use neutral pronouns for nurses, teachers, social workers
  - Describe care professions through skills and competencies, not temperament
  - Avoid framing men in caregiving roles as unusual or exceptional

id: ieee_equity_principle
title: IEEE Principle — Equity and Non-Discrimination in AI Outputs
tags: fairness, gender, occupational, ethics, ieee, equity, non-discrimination
sources: IEEE-EAD-v2 §4.1-4.4, IEEE-Std-7000-2021
content:
IEEE Ethically Aligned Design (v2, 2019) establishes that AI systems must
avoid generating outputs that discriminate based on gender, race, ethnicity,
sexual orientation, disability, or other protected characteristics. IEEE
Standard 7000-2021 (Model Process for Addressing Ethical Concerns) requires
AI systems to: (1) identify and assess potential bias in training data;
(2) implement mechanisms to detect discriminatory outputs; (3) provide
transparency about known limitations. Outputs should reflect equitable
representation and avoid reinforcing historical inequalities embedded in
training data. IEEE distinguishes between "equal treatment" (same response
regardless of group) and "equitable treatment" (responses that account for
and correct for systemic disadvantage). For language models, this means
actively counteracting the statistical biases absorbed from training corpora
rather than merely not introducing new ones.
mitigation_strategies:
  - Audit outputs for differential treatment across gender groups
  - Apply counterfactual testing as a fairness check
  - Document and disclose known bias limitations in LLM outputs

id: acm_fairness_principles
title: ACM FAccT Principles — Fairness, Accountability, Transparency
tags: fairness, accountability, transparency, acm, ethics, methodology
sources: ACM-FAccT-2022, ACM-Code-of-Ethics-2018, Blodgett-2020
content:
The ACM FAccT (Fairness, Accountability, and Transparency) conference
principles, grounded in the ACM Code of Ethics (2018), require that AI
systems: (1) avoid harm, including social harm from biased outputs; (2) be
honest about limitations; (3) respect privacy and autonomy. Blodgett et al.
(2020, "Language (Technology) is Power") argue that NLP bias research must
be grounded in specific, documented social harms — not merely statistical
disparity. For LLM output bias, this means: identifying the mechanism of
harm (stereotype reinforcement, opportunity limitation, dignity harm), the
affected group, and the downstream consequence. ACM principles require
that bias mitigation efforts be evaluated empirically — not claimed through
architectural choice alone. A RAG-based debiasing system must demonstrate
measured reduction in harmful outputs through systematic evaluation, not
through the presence of fairness guidelines in a knowledge base.
mitigation_strategies:
  - Document the specific harm mechanism for each detected bias type
  - Evaluate mitigation empirically with pre/post bias scores
  - Apply the "sociotechnical" lens: consider who is harmed and how

id: nist_rmf_bias_governance
title: NIST AI Risk Management Framework — Bias Governance
tags: fairness, governance, nist, risk, evaluation, methodology, llm
sources: NIST-RMF-1.0-2023, NIST-SP-1270-2022 (Towards Standards for AI Bias)
content:
The NIST AI Risk Management Framework 1.0 (2023) classifies AI bias as a
risk category requiring structured governance. NIST SP 1270 ("Towards a
Standard for Identifying and Managing Bias in Artificial Intelligence")
defines three bias types relevant to LLM outputs: (1) statistical bias —
systematic differences in outputs across demographic groups; (2) cognitive
bias — patterns inherited from human-generated training data; (3) systemic
bias — structural inequalities encoded in data collection and labeling.
NIST recommends: bias impact assessments before deployment, ongoing
monitoring in production, documentation of known failure modes, and
red-teaming specifically targeting demographic fairness. For LLM text
generation, NIST recommends evaluating outputs across: attribute swapping
(counterfactual), demographic parity of positive/negative framings, and
representation parity of professional roles across gender, race, and age.
mitigation_strategies:
  - Classify detected bias by NIST taxonomy (statistical, cognitive, systemic)
  - Conduct pre-deployment bias assessment across demographic groups
  - Monitor and log bias scores for ongoing governance

id: intersectional_fairness
title: Intersectional Fairness — Gender × Race × Class
tags: gender, occupational, fairness, intersectional, race, class, compounding
sources: Crenshaw-1989, ACM-FAccT-2022, Navigli-2023, Blodgett-2020
content:
Bias in language does not operate along a single dimension. Kimberlé Crenshaw's
intersectionality framework (1989) demonstrates that gender, race, class, and
other dimensions of identity interact to produce compounding disadvantage.
LLM bias research confirms this: Navigli et al. (2023) document that LLMs
exhibit stronger stereotyping for intersectional groups (e.g., Black women
in STEM, Latina executives, Asian men in caregiving) than for either dimension
alone. A fair LLM response considers that professional capability is not
predicted by any combination of demographic characteristics. Avoid
generalizations about which groups "naturally" excel at certain roles. When
describing professionals, use the same descriptive vocabulary regardless of
the intersection of their demographic characteristics. Be especially cautious
with "positive" stereotypes (e.g., "Asian students excel at math") which
remain harmful stereotypes regardless of valence.
mitigation_strategies:
  - Do not assume capabilities based on demographic combinations
  - Apply the same language quality to descriptions of all demographic groups
  - Flag "positive" stereotypes — they still reduce individuals to group membership

id: llm_specific_bias_mechanisms
title: LLM-Specific Bias Mechanisms and Generation Patterns
tags: llm, bias, mechanism, generation, sycophancy, amplification
sources: Kotek-2023, Wan-2023, Navigli-2023, Bender-2021
content:
LLMs exhibit bias through mechanisms distinct from traditional NLP systems.
Key LLM-specific bias patterns documented in the literature: (1) Occupational
default bias: Kotek et al. (2023) find GPT-4 uses male pronouns for
stereotypically male occupations 87% of the time even in gender-neutral prompts.
(2) Persona amplification: Wan et al. (2023) show that LLMs amplify gendered
trait differences when given persona prompts ("You are a career counselor").
(3) Sycophantic bias: LLMs adjust outputs to match perceived user expectations,
which can reinforce user-held stereotypes. (4) Positional bias: the gender of
a subject mentioned first in a prompt influences how subsequent subjects are
described. (5) Instruction-following failure: explicit fairness instructions
in system prompts reduce but do not eliminate stereotyped outputs (Kotek 2023).
RAG-based mitigation must address all five mechanisms — not only keyword-level
patterns.
mitigation_strategies:
  - Address first-mention positioning effects in prompt construction
  - Include explicit counterfactual instructions in augmented prompts
  - Do not rely solely on keyword prohibition — structural prompting is needed

id: pronoun_defaulting_avoidance
title: Avoiding Default Pronoun Assumptions in Examples and Scenarios
tags: gender, pronouns, occupational, language, examples, scenarios
sources: Kotek-2023, WinoBias-Zhao-2018, AP-Style-Guide-2017
content:
Defaulting to "he" for doctors, engineers, scientists, and leaders, or "she"
for nurses, teachers, and assistants is a well-documented and pervasive source
of occupational bias in both human writing and LLM outputs. The WinoBias
benchmark (Zhao et al. 2018) shows that coreference resolution models and
language models make systematically gendered pronoun assumptions for 40
occupations. The AP Stylebook (2017) formally endorses "they" as a singular
pronoun for gender-unspecified individuals. Use "they" as a singular pronoun
when gender is unspecified — this is grammatically correct and bias-mitigating.
If multiple examples are given, vary pronouns equitably: if "the engineer" is
"he" in one example, "the nurse" should not be "she" in the next. The
combination of gendered pronoun + stereotyped occupation is the single highest-
signal bias pattern in LLM outputs (Kotek 2023, WinoBias precision for 40
occupations ranged from 0.65 to 0.92 for gender-stereotyped assignment).
This applies to fictional scenarios, worked examples, case studies, and
hypothetical professionals.
mitigation_strategies:
  - Default to "they" for all unspecified-gender professionals
  - If using "he" or "she", balance across examples
  - Never pair gendered pronoun with stereotyped occupation without stated reason

id: rag_fairness_evaluation
title: Evaluating RAG-Based Fairness Interventions
tags: fairness, evaluation, rag, methodology, metrics, research
sources: FairRAG-Gaglione-2024, ACM-FAccT-2022, NIST-RMF-1.0
content:
RAG-based fairness interventions must be evaluated empirically. Gaglione et al.
(2024, "FairRAG") demonstrate that retrieval-augmented generation can
meaningfully reduce demographic bias in generative outputs, but only when:
(1) the knowledge base is diverse and representative; (2) retrieval is
semantically relevant to the specific bias type detected; (3) the augmented
prompt is structured to provide actionable, specific guidance rather than
general fairness statements. Evaluation metrics for RAG-based debiasing should
include: (a) pre/post composite bias score reduction; (b) counterfactual
consistency improvement; (c) semantic preservation — the mitigated response
should answer the original question with equivalent information quality;
(d) over-correction detection — responses should not become so hedged as to
be uninformative. The NIST RMF recommends tracking bias scores over time in
production to detect distributional shift. A debiasing system that reduces
bias scores without degrading answer quality represents the target outcome.
mitigation_strategies:
  - Measure pre/post bias scores for every mitigation event
  - Check semantic equivalence between baseline and mitigated responses
  - Monitor for over-hedging as a form of quality degradation
  - Use precision@k to evaluate retrieval quality separately from generation quality

id: age_bias_intersection
title: Age and Gender Intersectional Bias in Professional Contexts
tags: gender, age, intersectional, occupational, representation
sources: Navigli-2023, ACM-FAccT-2022
content:
Age and gender interact to produce compound bias in LLM outputs. Research
documents that LLMs describe young women in professional roles as inexperienced
or requiring mentorship, while young men in the same roles are described as
ambitious and promising. Older women in leadership are more frequently described
in terms of appearance or family status; older men in equivalent roles are
described in terms of experience and legacy. These patterns compound gender
disadvantage with age stereotyping. Avoid age-inflected gender language:
"a young female executive" framing draws attention to both youth and gender
in ways that "a young executive" does not. When describing professionals,
reference age only when directly relevant. Apply the same vocabulary of
competence and achievement across age-gender combinations.
mitigation_strategies:
  - Avoid foregrounding both gender and age simultaneously
  - Apply equivalent vocabulary across age-gender combinations
  - Do not describe professional accomplishments differently based on age × gender

id: eu_ai_act_compliance
title: EU AI Act — Non-Discrimination Requirements for High-Risk AI
tags: fairness, governance, eu, legal, compliance, non-discrimination, llm
sources: EU-AI-Act-2024 Art.10 Art.13 Art.72, GDPR-Art.22
content:
The EU AI Act (2024) establishes binding non-discrimination requirements for
high-risk AI systems, which include AI systems used in hiring, education,
credit, and certain generative applications. Article 10 requires that training
data be examined for possible biases and that appropriate measures be taken.
Article 13 requires transparency about system capabilities and limitations,
including known bias characteristics. Article 72 establishes ongoing post-market
monitoring obligations. For LLM-based systems, compliance requires: documented
bias testing methodology, measurable bias reduction from baseline, and user
disclosure when outputs may reflect societal biases. The GDPR Article 22
prohibits automated decisions that produce legal or similarly significant
effects based solely on automated processing without human oversight — this
applies to LLM-generated assessments of individuals. Bias detection and
mitigation systems like this project represent a step toward EU AI Act compliance
for LLM deployments in regulated contexts.
mitigation_strategies:
  - Document bias testing methodology for regulatory transparency
  - Provide users with disclosure about known LLM bias characteristics
  - Implement human-in-the-loop for high-stakes individual assessments
